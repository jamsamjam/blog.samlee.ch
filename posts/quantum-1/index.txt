<h3>An experiment with electrons</h3>
<p><img src="/posts/quantum-1/electron.png" alt="Electron Experiment"></p>
<p>It’s really difficult to set up this kind of experiment in a real-world setting — the apparatus would have to be built on an incredibly small scale. But thanks to many experiments that have been conducted over the years, we know how electrons behave in such scenarios.</p>
<p>What’s really fascinating is that electrons are <strong>particles</strong>, yet they behave like <strong>waves</strong>.<br>
Unlike water waves, they can’t be split or spread out, so they always arrive in lumps. So why do we still see interference?<br>
If you look at (c), we observe that when we close slit 1 (or 2) <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>P</mi><mn>12</mn></msub></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">P_{12}</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.8333em;vertical-align:-0.15em;"></span><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.13889em;">P</span><span class="msupsub"><span class="vlist-t vlist-t2"><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.3011em;"><span style="top:-2.55em;margin-left:-0.1389em;margin-right:0.05em;"><span class="pstrut" style="height:2.7em;"></span><span class="sizing reset-size6 size3 mtight"><span class="mord mtight"><span class="mord mtight">12</span></span></span></span></span><span class="vlist-s">​</span></span><span class="vlist-r"><span class="vlist" style="height:0.15em;"><span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span></span> is sometimes higher than the sum, or sometimes less. In other words, opening or closing a slit increase or decreases the intensity. So we say electrons are <em>sometimes like a particle and sometimes like a wave</em>.</p>

<p><br>
We've seen how electrons, despite being particles, show interference patterns. But why do electrons create the patterns? They don't just take one path. Instead, they <em>explore all possible paths</em>, and each path contributes a probability amplitude. These amplitudes interfere — sometimes constructively, sometimes destructively — which leads to the observed pattern.</p>
<p>But this idea of sum over paths isn't limited to electrons. It applies to photons (particles of light) as well.This is a core idea of quantum theory, often described as Feynman’s <em>sum over histories</em>. A great video that gives you an intuition about this (and quantum physics) can be found <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KTzGBJPuJwM">here</a>.</p>
