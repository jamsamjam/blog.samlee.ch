1.2. Block Multiplication

Suppose we have two square matrices AAA and BBB of size n×nn \times nn×n. Guess whay? We can multiply them block by block!

A=[A1A2A3A4],B=[B1B2B3B4]A = \begin{bmatrix} A_1 & A_2 \\ A_3 & A_4 \end{bmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{bmatrix} B_1 & B_2 \\ B_3 & B_4 \end{bmatrix}A=[A1​A3​​A2​A4​​],B=[B1​B3​​B2​B4​​] AB=[A1B1+A2B3A1B2+A2B4A3B1+A4B3A3B2+A4B4]AB = \begin{bmatrix} A_1B_1 + A_2B_3 & A_1B_2 + A_2B_4 \\ A_3B_1 + A_4B_3 & A_3B_2 + A_4B_4 \end{bmatrix}AB=[A1​B1​+A2​B3​A3​B1​+A4​B3​​A1​B2​+A2​B4​A3​B2​+A4​B4​​]

It may not be very straightforward to see why this works, but you can see it works by hand. I think it's enough here to know that we can do this.

1.3. Inverses (Square Matrix)

1.3.1. Existence of Inverse

Not all matrices have inverses. If A−1A^{-1}A−1 exists (= invertible, non-singular), ..

AA−1=A−1A=IAA^{-1} = A^{-1}A = IAA−1=A−1A=I

Why a matrix wouldn't have an inverse (singular) ?

(1326)\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 6 \end{pmatrix}(12​36​)

  1. Determinant of this matrix is zero.

  2. Suppose there is an inverse matrix A−1A^{-1}A−1, then when we multiply AAA by A−1A^{-1}A−1, the result should be combination of the columns of AAA. But they lie on the same line, so we can never get the identity matrix III.

  3. If we can find a vector xxx such that Ax=0Ax = 0Ax=0, then AAA is singular. In this case, x=(3,−1)x = (3, -1)x=(3,−1).

(1326)(3−1)=(00)\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 6 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}(12​36​)(3−1​)=(00​)

Why this is an issue for an inverse?

Ax=0Ax = 0Ax=0 A−1Ax=x=0\begin{align*} A^{-1} A x &= x = 0 \end{align*}A−1Ax​=x=0​

But xxx was not zero!

Important!


If a combination of the columns of AAA can give you the zero vector, then AAA is singular and does not have an inverse. In other words, if there is a non-zero vector xxx such that Ax=0Ax = 0Ax=0, then AAA is singular.

1.3.2. Gauss-Jordan

(1327)(acbd)=(1001)\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 7 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a & c \\ b & d \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}(12​37​)(ab​cd​)=(10​01​)

Finding the inverse is like solving the system.

A×column j of A−1=column j of IA \times \text{column } j \text{ of } A^{-1} = \text{column } j \text{ of } IA×column j of A−1=column j of I

Gauss-Jordan can solve two equations at once.

(1327)(ab)=(10)\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 7 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}(12​37​)(ab​)=(10​) (1327)(cd)=(01)\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 7 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c \\ d \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}(12​37​)(cd​)=(01​)

Jordan once said, we can..

(13∣1027∣01)\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 & | & 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 7 & | & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}(12​37​∣∣​10​01​) →(13∣1001∣−21)\rightarrow \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 & | & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & | & -2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}→(10​31​∣∣​1−2​01​)

Now that we have the upper triangular, Gauss would have said to quit, but Jordan said keep going!

→(10∣7−301∣−21)\rightarrow \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & | & 7 & -3 \\ 0 & 1 & | & -2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}→(10​01​∣∣​7−2​−31​)

A−1=(7−3−21)A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 & -3 \\ -2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}A−1=(7−2​−31​)
